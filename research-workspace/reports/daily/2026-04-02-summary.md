# Daily Idea Summary: 2026-04-02

## Headline
Strong day for NPU-grounded systems papers; the clearest idea opportunities are where runtime/fusion/speculation policies are hardware-relevant but still mostly rule-based, empirical, or missing explicit anti-fusion/anti-optimization logic.

## Top Takeaways
- DVM and AscendOptimizer are the highest-value reads: both attack real NPU compiler/runtime bottlenecks, but neither yet seems to give a fully interpretable microarchitecture-grounded decision policy for fusion/scheduling/tiling.
- Several papers show that the real optimization objective is not just kernel speed: compile latency (DVM), recompilation/update cost (Orion), backend legality (EAGLE-Pangu), and sustained thermal/power behavior (edge LLM study) can change the right graph partition or fusion boundary.
- A recurring opportunity is to turn hardware-aware engineering into explicit cost models: many papers are clearly shaped by real NPU constraints, but stop short of predicting when to fuse, speculate, tile, or split.
- The most promising research gap across today’s set is negative policy design: when not to fuse, when not to speculate, when not to map to the NPU, when not to use a larger fused/verification region, and when compile/update overhead dominates execution gains.
- There is strong evidence that memory movement and pipeline structure matter more than operator-count reduction alone: speculative decoding, host/kernel co-optimization, ANE weight patching, and codec-assisted graph reshaping all point to memory- and orchestration-aware compiler decisions as the next step.

## Good Problem, Weak Solution
- DVM: excellent problem—dynamic-shape/runtime compilation on NPUs—but current fusion story appears opportunity-expanding rather than profitability-explaining; missing anti-fusion and VM-vs-native scheduling analysis.
- AscendOptimizer: excellent problem—host tiling plus kernel scheduling on Ascend—but solution seems search/retrieval-heavy and only partly explanatory; likely lacks an explicit bottleneck model or stop policy.
- Orion: excellent problem—ANE programmability plus recompilation bottlenecks—but optimization contribution is more enablement/patching than a principled compiler decision framework for partitioning/fusion/scheduling.
- EAGLE-Pangu: excellent backend-realism problem—making speculative tree decoding safe on Ascend—but solution is mostly a correctness/portability bridge, not yet a hardware-aware optimization policy.
- OpenPangu speculative decoding: good NPU memory-wall problem, but likely still missing a predictive model of acceptance/verification/KV-cache tradeoffs and when speculation should be disabled.
- TinyML CubeSat pipeline: good tiny-NPU deployment problem, but 'hardware-aware mapping' appears under-specified and likely too coarse for compiler insight.
- ANVIL: strong hardware-mismatch problem statement—remove NPU-hostile operators—but solution is graph redesign rather than a compiler policy; still leaves open when selective reintroduction of 'bad' ops would be worth it.

## Worth Further Thought
- DVM: best follow-up direction is a runtime fusion cost model that includes decode overhead, on-chip memory pressure, and pipeline overlap limits; also a tiered VM-to-native promotion path for hot shapes.
- AscendOptimizer: likely the richest seed for a joint fusion/tiling/scheduling project on real NPUs; especially promising is learning a stop/avoid policy from the ~50% of operators that do not beat references.
- Orion: compelling substrate for recompilation-aware graph partitioning and no-fusion regions on ANE, where update/reload costs may dominate normal execution-centric optimization.
- Speculative decoding papers (EAGLE-Pangu and OpenPangu): good opening for treating speculation as a graph partitioning/fusion/scheduling problem instead of only a model/runtime policy.
- ANVIL: worth mining for 'bad operator families' and for the broader compiler idea that some graphs should be rewritten around hardware capabilities, not merely lowered better.
- Edge sustained-load study: useful motivation for thermal-/power-/bandwidth-aware fusion and scheduling; especially interesting if peak-optimal kernels are not sustained-optimal.
- Cross-paper idea: a compiler objective that jointly models execution latency, compile latency, update latency, memory traffic, and hardware state could unify several of today’s papers.

## Recurring Themes
- Real NPU constraints are finally foregrounded: Ascend, ANE, Hailo, mobile NPUs, and tiny MCU+NPU targets all expose target-specific legality and performance boundaries that generic graph compiler heuristics miss.
- Compiler/runtime decisions are increasingly shaped by non-execution costs: compile time, recompilation/update cost, cache management complexity, fallback/debug paths, and sustained thermal behavior.
- Most systems are good at finding more opportunities to optimize, but weak at saying when optimization should stop; explicit anti-fusion/anti-speculation/anti-mapping reasoning is rare.
- Memory-system effects dominate: KV-cache traffic, host-device movement, on-chip buffer pressure, bandwidth ceilings, and layout constraints recur more often than pure compute bottlenecks.
- Pipeline-aware reasoning is present implicitly but rarely formalized: host/kernel alternation, branch/commit cache flows, decode/verify stages, and decode+NPU pipelines all suggest scheduling structure matters beyond operator fusion.
- Several papers effectively reshape the graph to fit hardware rather than optimizing a fixed graph—bytecode VM execution, codec-MV priors, weight patching, and speculative decode structures all point in this direction.
